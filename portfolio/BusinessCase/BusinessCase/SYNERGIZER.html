Why the Synergizer IS Project Failed — And What We Can Learn From It

A concise post-mortem of MarketSource's Synergizer initiative: causes, 5 Whys analyses, and pragmatic recommendations.

Overview

When MarketSource launched the Synergizer project, the mission was bold: build a fully customized information system to manage a growing global workforce, improve collaboration, and eliminate inefficiencies. The ambition was admirable — but the end result was a system that users refused to adopt, financial losses, and ultimately a full return to the old processes.

Understanding why Synergizer failed requires looking beyond surface-level problems. Below we break down the core failure factors with 5 Whys root-cause analyses and conclude with lessons MarketSource must carry forward.

1. Budget & Schedule: Delivered on Time but Over Budget

The project impressively met its deadline — but at a steep cost. Synergizer exceeded its budget by 20%, primarily due to the sudden departure of a key resource and the urgent need to recruit two replacements.

5 Whys: Budget & Schedule Failure
  1. Why did the project exceed budget?
    Because two additional resources were hired urgently.
  2. Why was urgent hiring needed?
    A key employee left mid-project.
  3. Why did this departure cause major disruption?
    There was no contingency plan for critical personnel.
  4. Why was there no contingency plan?
    Risk management practices were not formally implemented.
  5. Why were risk management practices missing?
    The project manager lacked formal PM training, and the company relied on informal experience rather than structured methodologies.

Conclusion: The cost overrun reflects deep structural weaknesses in planning and risk management, not a single isolated event.

2. Quality of the Information System: Technically Correct, Practically Wrong

Technically, Synergizer worked and fulfilled the documented requirements. But users found it confusing, unintuitive, and misaligned with their workflow. That mismatch led directly to rejection.

5 Whys: Poor Quality of the Delivered System
  1. Why did the system not meet user expectations?
    It wasn’t aligned with their daily workflow or usability needs.
  2. Why wasn’t it aligned?
    Users were not adequately involved in design or testing.
  3. Why were users not involved?
    No user-centered design process or feedback loop was implemented.
  4. Why wasn’t a user-centered approach used?
    The team focused on technical requirements rather than user experience.
  5. Why was UX overlooked?
    The in-house team lacked experience with internal systems and the project lacked agile methods that could have incorporated ongoing feedback.

Conclusion: Technical correctness couldn’t compensate for poor human-centered design.

3. Profitability: Expected Savings Never Materialized

One of Synergizer’s goals was to reduce financial waste — especially inaccurate time reporting by virtual employees. Because the system was quickly abandoned, none of the intended cost savings were achieved; the project created new expenses and contributed to the loss of a key client.

5 Whys: Profitability Failure
  1. Why didn’t the project improve profitability?
    Users rejected the system, so cost-saving features weren’t utilized.
  2. Why did users reject the system?
    It felt unfamiliar, complex, and disruptive.
  3. Why was it unfamiliar and complex?
    Users weren’t involved and received little training.
  4. Why weren’t they involved or trained?
    No change management plan was implemented.
  5. Why wasn’t change management implemented?
    Leadership underestimated how crucial user engagement is to IS success.

Conclusion: Profitability was never given a credible chance to appear because adoption failed early.

4. Communication: The Silent Killer of the Project

Communication failures permeated Synergizer. Stakeholders were not informed, user concerns were not captured, and virtual teams were not engaged in meaningful conversations — leaving users confused, fearful, and distrustful.

5 Whys: Communication Failure
  1. Why was communication ineffective?
    No structured communication plan existed.
  2. Why wasn’t there a communication plan?
    The project manager didn’t prioritize it during planning.
  3. Why wasn’t communication prioritized?
    Technical delivery overshadowed stakeholder engagement.
  4. Why was stakeholder engagement undervalued?
    Leadership assumed users would naturally adapt to the new system.
  5. Why did they assume this?
    The organization lacked a culture of formal project and change management.

Conclusion: Poor communication created resistance and undermined adoption.

5. User Adoption: The Central Failure Factor

Everything collapsed at the point of user adoption. Virtual employees, managers, and even executives refused to use the system because:

  • The system was unfamiliar
  • Workflows didn’t translate smoothly
  • Training was insufficient
  • Communication was unclear
  • Some feared the system would expose inflated working hours

In short: no user adoption → no business value → project failure.

What MarketSource Must Do Next: Action Recommendations

To avoid repeating Synergizer’s fate, MarketSource should adopt the following actions:

1. Implement Formal PM Training

Equip project managers with PMBOK, Agile, or hybrid methodologies.

2. Create Detailed Risk & Resource Contingency Plans

Budget buffers, resource backup plans, and clear risk triggers are essential.

3. Adopt User-Centered Design

Engage end-users early and throughout development; run iterative testing.

4. Deploy a Full Change Management Strategy

Communicate early, train thoroughly, and prepare people for change (ADKAR/Kotter patterns). [Image of ADKAR Change Management Model]

5. Establish Two-Way Communication Channels

Support transparent updates, user feedback loops, and active stakeholder engagement.

6. Encourage Cross-Functional Collaboration

Close coordination between developers, users, and leadership ensures alignment.

Conclusion: A Failure That Can Become a Turning Point

The Synergizer project failed not because of technology, but because of people, process, and communication. MarketSource now has a unique opportunity to transform its project culture — if it applies the lessons learned.

With structured methodology, user-centered practices, and a stronger focus on communication and change management, future IS projects can achieve the value Synergizer could not.
